Fraud Awareness
Two Patterns · Cyber411.ai Reference
The Patient Attack & The Display Name Trick
Wire Fraud

Two scenarios from Cyber411.ai's awareness library come up consistently in title-adjacent contexts, and both are worth walking through with your team rather than just forwarding as bullet points.

Pattern 1 — The Patient Attack
  • A fraudster gains access to a real email account and watches for two to three weeks — learning who talks to whom and the transaction timeline.
  • Just before closing, they send updated wire instructions from the legitimate, compromised account. No spoofed domain. No fake display name. It's the real email.
  • It passes most people's instinctive scan because there's nothing visually wrong.
Pattern 2 — The Display Name Trick
  • The email shows a familiar display name: "Sarah Johnson, First National Title." Everything looks normal at a glance.
  • The actual sending address is sarah.johnson.fnttitle@gmail.com — not the company domain. Almost nobody checks the actual address unless they've been trained to.
What Stops Both of These
  • Verbally verify all wire instructions using a number already in your records — not a number included in the email itself.
  • Inspect the actual sending address on any email that includes wire instructions. It takes three seconds. Do it every time.
  • Any mid-transaction change to wire details should trigger a direct phone call before anything else happens.

Fraud Awareness
Active & Documented Pattern
Wire Fraud Is Getting Smarter — The AI-Assisted Attack
Elevated Risk

The same AI that makes the tools above fast and convincing is now being used to make wire fraud harder to detect. This isn't theoretical. The pattern below describes an active, documented attack methodology appearing more frequently in real estate transactions.

How the Attack Sequence Works
  • Weeks 1–3, surveillance only: The attacker compromises an email account and does nothing visible. They study communication patterns, transaction timelines, and who has authority over wire decisions.
  • Week 4, the strike: AI drafts an email using the agent's exact tone and language, references the correct property, and sends it at 4 PM the day before closing — when pressure is highest and scrutiny is lowest.
  • The spoofing layer: The display name shows a trusted person. The sending domain is a near-match — wellsfarg0.com instead of wellsfargo.com.
  • The urgency frame: "Must fund today." The time pressure is engineered — it's the mechanism that discourages verification.
What Actually Stops This
  • Call to verify any wire changes using a number already in your records — not one from the email.
  • Hover the sender display name and inspect the real address. Three seconds, every time.
  • Educate clients about wire fraud at the start of the transaction — at first contact, not the day before close.
  • Re-verify wire details immediately before funding, even if they were confirmed the day prior.

Case Review — Actual File
Seller Impersonation, POA Fraud & Identity Fabrication
Documented Case

This scenario came from a real file review. A seller inquiry arrived, documents followed promptly, and the transaction started moving like a normal cash close. What the review eventually surfaced was a layered impersonation attempt.

How the File Developed
  • Opening: Seller claims power of attorney on behalf of the owner and requests a fast cash close. The property is vacant with no mortgage — a profile that simplifies the mechanics of this kind of fraud.
  • Documents arrive quickly: A low-resolution driver's license. A notarized POA. Wire instructions routing to a foreign account. Seller is overseas and unavailable for in-person contact.
  • Verification declined: A request for a video call is refused. A 14-day close window creates pressure to keep moving.
  • What the review found: No POA recorded with the county. Document alteration detected on the submitted ID. A fraud risk scan returned a score of 87 out of 100.
  • Outcome: File escalated before closing. Nothing recorded. No funds moved.
The Pattern — When Several of These Appear Together
  • Remote seller with an urgent, compressed close window
  • POA claim with no county record to verify it
  • Low-quality or potentially altered identity documents
  • Resistance when live verification is requested
  • Wire instructions routing to a foreign account
  • Vacant, free-and-clear property with an absent owner
ATS Observations on This Case
  • None of these flags would have been alarming in isolation. The pattern only became clear when viewed together — which is why fraud scoring tools and a second-reviewer culture matter.
  • Vacant, free-and-clear properties are a known fraud target profile. This type of file warrants extra scrutiny as a standard practice.
  • A fast-moving file and a clean-looking file are not the same thing.

Fraud Awareness
Documented Pattern · Increasing Frequency
Fake Payoff Statement Fraud — The Lien That Never Closed
Critical Risk

A fraudster intercepts a transaction, builds a counterfeit payoff statement using the correct loan number, borrower name, and lender letterhead — and swaps the wire routing account for their own. The deal funds. The money moves. The real lien stays open, because the real servicer never received anything.

Nobody realizes something is wrong until the payoff doesn't post. By then, the funds are gone.

Why This Is Difficult to Catch Before Funding
  • The document looks correct. Loan number, borrower name, lender letterhead — all right. The only thing wrong is the routing account.
  • The domain is close but not identical. A digit replaces a letter. A hyphen is added. It passes a quick glance.
  • The urgency framing is engineered. "Must fund today." The pressure is deliberate — designed to discourage taking an extra step.
  • The specificity builds false confidence. Because the fraudster accessed prior communication, they can reference the correct parties, property, and timeline.
What Stops This
  • Call the servicer to verify payoff amounts and wire details using a number from your own records — not the number printed on the document you just received.
  • Any payoff statement that arrives through unexpected channels warrants a direct call before anything else.
  • Mid-transaction wire account changes are red flags by definition. Verify independently, regardless of how official the document looks.
  • Urgency framing is a tactic. A few minutes of verification is less expensive than an uncured lien and a missing wire.
